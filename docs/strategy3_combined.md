##Codex Top 3 Agents##
1. Post-Earnings Drift Continuation / PEAD (Claude): This is the strongest single addition because it is a clean complement to the existing pre-earnings `catalyst_confluence` strategy. It trades after the report, so it avoids the binary earnings event and much of the pre-event IV crush risk while still using a well-researched anomaly. Claude's version is especially implementation-ready because it gives a public Finviz URL, avoids unsupported Finviz filters, uses existing yfinance/Finnhub/Alpaca paths, and keeps v1 to long calls in a non-tech and mid-cap-biased subset where the PEAD edge is more defensible. The main risk is PEAD decay, but the strategy has the best mix of evidence, infrastructure fit, and near-term buildability.

2. Activist 13D Follow-Through (Codex): This is the best truly new event-driven strategy in the set. It adds an official SEC-filing signal that is not another momentum, earnings, or Finviz-only screen, and it naturally pushes the pipeline toward non-tech opportunities where activist campaigns are often more relevant. The short repricing window after a fresh active Schedule 13D fits 14-28 DTE long calls, and the data source is public and auditable. It scores slightly below PEAD because it needs new structured SEC parsing, filing metadata, event scoring, and careful stale-gap handling, but the orthogonality and signal quality justify the extra implementation work.

3. Non-Tech Sector Relative Strength (Claude): This is the best sector-first technical strategy because it is simple, stateless, public-data friendly, and directly addresses the current system's lack of top-down sector context. The regime gate is important: requiring the leading non-tech sector ETF to be above its 50-day SMA and up at least 2% over four weeks prevents the strategy from forcing trades in flat or weak markets. It does overlap somewhat with `coiled_setup` and Kimi's sector momentum idea, so I would not choose both, but Claude's version is cleaner and easier to test because the edge is sector alignment first and single-stock momentum second.

##Claude Top 3 Agents##

1. Post-Earnings Drift Continuation / PEAD (Claude): This is the single strongest addition because it is the rare strategy where every dimension lines up. It is a near-perfect complement to the existing pre-earnings `catalyst_confluence` because it deliberately trades after the report, which sidesteps the binary earnings-day gap risk and the IV-crush risk that long-premium pre-earnings positions inherit. The underlying anomaly (Bernard & Thomas 1989; Garfinkel, Hribar & Hsiao 2024) is one of the most replicated short-horizon results in academic finance, and the spec is honest about where the edge still survives in 2026 — mid-cap, non-tech, with a meaningful surprise and a confirmed day-1 reaction. The Finviz URL is verified, the post-filters (≥5% surprise, ≥3% day-1 reaction, non-tech sector, $300M–$10B market cap) are concrete and testable, and the data path is 100% reuse of yfinance + Finnhub + Alpha Vantage that the system already has wired. Compared with Codex's PEAD spec, Claude's version is materially more implementation-ready: explicit thresholds, explicit sub-universe, defers the bearish variant to v2, and uses the only Finviz `earningsdate_prevweek` filter that the panel verified actually works. The risk is real PEAD decay, but even in the worst case the strategy degrades to empty batches rather than producing noise — the post-filters fail closed.

2. Activist 13D Follow-Through (Codex): This is the most orthogonal signal in the entire 9-strategy set. Every other proposal is either a momentum overlay (Kimi's sector, Kimi's commodity, Claude's sector RS), a technical pattern (Kimi's spring-loaded), or an earnings event (the PEAD pair). 13D follow-through is none of those — it is a structured corporate-event signal off an official SEC filing, and it naturally pulls the candidate pool toward non-tech (industrials, consumer, energy, financials, healthcare services) where activist campaigns are concentrated. The 2024 beneficial-ownership modernization tightened the SC 13D initial filing window to five business days, which keeps the signal fresh enough to actionably trade with 14–28 DTE long calls before the first repricing wave completes. Codex's spec is detailed enough to build: explicit tiering by filing age, Item 4 active-intent filter, explicit exclusion of passive 13G ownership, gap-exhaustion and earnings-collision penalties, and a clear event-score formula. The honest cost is that it needs a structured SEC parser the codebase does not yet have and the candidate count can be lumpy on quiet weeks — but those are exactly the right tradeoffs to accept for a signal that genuinely diversifies away from the existing momentum + earnings axis.

3. Non-Tech Sector Relative Strength (Claude): This is the right third pick because it closes the one structural gap that the existing two strategies leave open — neither `catalyst_confluence` nor `coiled_setup` has any top-down sector view, and Strategy B's biggest known failure mode is picking a strong single stock inside a deteriorating sector. The spec is simple in the right way: rank nine non-tech sector SPDRs (XLE, XLF, XLI, XLV, XLU, XLP, XLY, XLB, XLRE) by 4-week return, then run a dynamic Finviz query inside the leading sector. The regime gate is the load-bearing piece — refusing to trade unless the top ETF is above its 50-day SMA AND the dispersion is ≥ +2% — and it is what makes me prefer this over Kimi's Sector Momentum Ignition. Kimi's version stacks more momentum-acceleration filters on top of the same idea, but those extra filters do not fix the real failure mode of sector momentum, which is trading into a downtrend or a flat market; Claude's regime gate does. The strategy overlaps modestly with `coiled_setup` since both end up favoring trending names, but the sector-first logic and the explicit non-tech bias mean it will pick up rotations (energy leadership, financial leadership, healthcare leadership) that `coiled_setup` misses because it screens single stocks without sector context. I am deliberately not picking Form 4 / Insider Cluster (either author's version) because Claude itself deferred its own variant to Appendix A and Codex's variant carries the heaviest implementation burden of the SEC-event family for a signal whose post-disclosure edge is the most decayed of the three event ideas considered.

##Kimi Top 3 Agents##

1. Post-Earnings Drift Continuation / PEAD (Claude): This is the single strongest addition because it is the rare strategy where every dimension lines up. It directly complements the existing pre-earnings `catalyst_confluence` by trading after the report, sidestepping binary gap risk and IV crush while still exploiting one of the most replicated short-horizon anomalies in academic finance (Bernard & Thomas 1989; Garfinkel, Hribar & Hsiao 2024). Claude's version is exceptionally honest about where the edge survives in 2026 — mid-cap, non-tech, with a meaningful surprise and a confirmed day-1 reaction — and the post-filters (EPS surprise >= 5%, day-1 reaction >= +3%, non-tech, $300M-$10B market cap) are precisely calibrated to that subset. The Finviz URL uses only confirmed-working filters, the data path is 100% reuse of existing yfinance/Finnhub/Alpaca wiring, and the strategy degrades to empty batches rather than producing noise if the filters do not fire. Compared with Codex's PEAD spec, Claude's version is more implementation-ready because it gives exact thresholds, defers the bearish variant to v2, and avoids the broader "any recent earnings" approach that risks including low-conviction names. It is the best evidence-to-complexity ratio in the entire set.

2. Non-Tech Sector Relative Strength (Claude): This is the cleanest sector-first strategy because it adds top-down diversification without over-engineering. The two-step screen — rank nine non-tech SPDRs by 4-week return, then run a dynamic Finviz query inside the leading sector — is elegant, stateless, and uses only yfinance plus the existing Finviz browser path. The regime gate is the load-bearing feature: requiring the top sector ETF to be above its 50-day SMA AND up at least 2% over four weeks prevents the strategy from forcing low-quality trades in flat or deteriorating markets. This directly addresses the known weakness in Strategy B (picking strong single stocks inside weak sectors) and captures sector rotations that the existing pipeline misses entirely. I rate it above Kimi's Sector Momentum Ignition because Claude's version is simpler and more robust — Kimi's additional momentum-acceleration filters (RSI slope, volume ratio, beta check) add complexity without fixing the core failure mode of trading into a downtrend, whereas Claude's regime gate does.

3. Sector Momentum Ignition (Kimi): This is the strongest of Kimi's three proposals and the best momentum strategy overall. The dual-momentum thesis (sector ETF momentum plus individual stock momentum acceleration) is academically robust and well-diversified from the existing technical coiled setup. I particularly value the risk-management layer: the SPY > SMA50 filter guards against momentum crashes per Daniel & Moskowitz (2016), the VIX > 30 filter reduces size or skips, and the sector concentration limit prevents overexposure. The implementation pattern mirrors existing services (Finviz screen plus yfinance validation), making it low-friction to build. It loses a small edge to Claude's Sector RS because it is more complex (multiple yfinance filters per candidate, composite scoring) and thus slightly more fragile, but the core idea is excellent, the non-tech focus is exactly what the pipeline needs, and the strategy will produce five candidates reliably when market conditions are favorable. It rounds out the top three as the best momentum complement to the PEAD and sector-RS picks.
